## Summary

A philosophical and practical essay on what happens to human expertise when AI commoditises the information-holding component. Argues that expertise was always made of four things — information, judgment, integrated attention, and accountability — and AI has only automated the first. The paradox: AI makes expertise more accessible to non-experts while making genuine expertise more valuable.

---

## Core Argument

**What expertise was always made of:**
1. **Information** — now commoditised by AI
2. **Judgment** — knowing which information matters in a specific situation
3. **Integrated attention** — pattern recognition that notices what doesn't fit
4. **Accountability** — standing behind a decision with your name and record
5. **Relationship** — expressed in timing, the right question, knowing when not to speak

*AI has commoditised #1. It cannot replicate #2–5.*

---

## The Paradox

- AI makes the ability to find answers less valuable
- But it makes the ability to ask the right questions considerably MORE valuable
- Knowing what to ask requires a mental model of the domain — which AI doesn't teach by doing the work for you
- **Senior experts with AI get better faster. Junior staff who use AI to avoid developing judgment become competent at AI, not at their field.**

---

## What Is Genuinely at Risk

- The traditional novice-to-expert pathway (writing memos, doing literature reviews) created capability through difficulty
- If AI handles this scaffolding, the question is: how do novices develop into experts?
- Not an argument against AI tools — but a call to redesign learning: from recall → evaluation, from answering → interrogating

---

## Skills That Survive (and Grow More Valuable)

- **Curiosity:** Making unexpected connections, pursuing questions for their own interest
- **Communication:** Understanding what someone actually needs (not just answering grammatically)
- **Care:** Investment in outcomes that comes from stakes — humans perform differently when something matters

---

## The Warning

A version of AI adoption exists where automation keeps expanding inward — delegating judgment, evaluation, and decision — until the human in the loop is mostly approving outputs without understanding when they're wrong. That endpoint is "the appearance of expertise" on a foundation that isn't there.
