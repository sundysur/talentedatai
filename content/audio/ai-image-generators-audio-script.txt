In 2026, there are four AI image generators worth your time — but they're not competing for the same crown. Adobe Firefly, Midjourney, ChatGPT's built-in image generation, and Flux have each become the best tool for a completely different type of person, and choosing the wrong one means paying for something that doesn't actually fit how you work.

Let's start with the thing most people want to know first: which one makes the best-looking images? The honest answer is Midjourney, and it's not particularly close at the top end. Version 7 produces images with a depth, texture, and compositional intelligence that still isn't matched by anything else. Skin, fabric, lighting — the details that make an AI image look like something you'd actually use professionally. The catch is there's no free trial, it starts at ten dollars a month, and there's a real learning curve to getting the most out of it. But if visual quality is your priority, Midjourney is where you end up.

Now here's the thing that trips a lot of people up about OpenAI's image generation: DALL-E 3 no longer exists as a standalone product. Image generation from OpenAI is now built directly into ChatGPT — it runs on a model called gpt-image-1 — and you access it through your ChatGPT subscription, not a separate tool. If you're already on ChatGPT Plus at twenty dollars a month, image generation is just there, included. The upside is that the conversational workflow is genuinely the easiest of any tool in this category — you describe what you want in plain English, refine it in plain English, and you never have to learn any special syntax. The downside is that if you want images and nothing else, twenty dollars a month for ChatGPT Plus is harder to justify than ten dollars a month for a dedicated Midjourney subscription.

Adobe Firefly has a different value proposition entirely, and it's one that doesn't get talked about enough. Firefly was trained exclusively on licensed Adobe Stock content, which means Adobe will actually back you up legally if anyone ever questions the copyright status of an image you generated with it. No other major AI image generator offers that. For anyone doing commercial work — agency designers, marketing teams, anyone creating content for clients — that matters more than it might seem. Firefly is also free to try at firefly.adobe.com, and if you're already an Adobe Creative Cloud subscriber, it's included.

The fourth option, Flux, is the one developers and technically confident users should know about. It's an open-weight model from Black Forest Labs, which means the model weights are publicly available — you can run it locally on your own machine, or access it through cloud APIs at roughly two to five cents per image. The photorealism it produces is genuinely competitive with Midjourney, and for anyone doing high-volume work or building image generation into an application, the economics are completely different from a subscription model. The trade-off is setup complexity. This is not the tool you point a non-technical person at and expect them to get going in ten minutes.

So here's how to think about the verdict. If image quality is the priority and you're prepared to put in the learning time, go with Midjourney. If you're doing commercial work and care about IP safety, Adobe Firefly is the professional default. If you're already on ChatGPT Plus and want image generation without another subscription, it's already in your account — use it. And if you're a developer or a high-volume creator who needs flexibility and cost efficiency, Flux is what you should be looking at.

The bigger point is that these four tools have stopped competing with each other on the same dimension. Midjourney is a quality product. Firefly is a safety product. ChatGPT Image is a convenience feature for ChatGPT subscribers. Flux is infrastructure. Picking the right one isn't about finding the overall winner — it's about knowing which of those four things matters most for the work you're actually doing.
